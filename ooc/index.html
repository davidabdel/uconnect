O
1. Outcomes — What do I really want?
Get clear on the result before you judge the choices.
O
What to do
  • Write the top 3–5 outcomes you want from this decision.
  • Rank them in order of importance.
  • Separate must-haves from nice-to-haves.
Example Choosing a hotel: 1) clean & family-friendly, 2) relaxing pool, 3) easy dinner options, 4) good value, 5) walkable to town.
Step 2 of 6
O
2. Options — What are all my choices?
More options usually creates better decisions.
O
What to do
  • List every realistic option — don't filter yet.
  • Add at least one creative or hybrid option.
  • Quantity first. Judgment comes later.
Example Stay where I am, move hotels, change city, split the stay, spend more for convenience, or cut the trip short.
Step 3 of 6
C
3. Consequences — What happens if I choose each option?
Look at the upside, downside, cost, risk, and long-term effect.
C
Questions to answer
  • What is the best thing that could happen?
  • What is the worst thing that could happen?
  • What will this cost in money, energy, stress, or time?
  • How will I feel about this in 1 week, 6 months, and 5 years?
Step 4 of 6
E
4. Evaluate — Which option best meets the outcome?
Score each option instead of relying on emotion alone.
E
Option Outcome Fit /10 Cost /10 Ease /10 Risk /10 Total /40

Score each column out of 10. Totals calculate automatically. Higher = better for Outcome Fit, Ease; lower = better for Cost, Risk — adjust your weighting mentally.

Step 5 of 6
M
5. Mitigate — How can I reduce the downsides?
Before rejecting the best option, see if the risks can be lowered.
M
What to do
  • Pick the leading option from step 4.
  • Write its biggest risks or objections.
  • Create a practical fix or workaround for each one.
Example Risk: hotel is too far from restaurants. Fix: book two nights near town, arrange transport in advance, use resort dining other nights.
